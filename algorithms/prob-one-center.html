1-Center — Smallest Enclosing Circle

Where do you stand so that the farthest person is as close as possible? Find the centre of the smallest circle that covers every point.

📄 Source: Alg06 · §7 (1-Center / smallest enclosing circle), Fig 7-16, Fig 7-18, Fig 7-23.

🧠 Solved by: Prune-and-Search

The problem

You are given n points scattered on a flat plane — think of them as houses on a map. You must choose one location, the centre, and the cost of that choice is the distance to the point farthest from it. The 1-center problem asks: pick the centre that makes this farthest distance as small as possible.

Equivalently — and this is the picture to hold onto — find the smallest circle that encloses all n points. The circle's centre is the answer; its radius is exactly that smallest-possible farthest distance. Every point lies inside or on the circle, and you can't make the circle any smaller without leaving a point out.

The key phrase is min-max: you minimise the maximum distance. Define the terms carefully:

  • Centre — the single point you get to place anywhere on the plane.
  • Radius — the distance from your centre to the farthest of the n points. This, and only this, is what you pay.
  • min-max — you are not minimising the total or the average distance. Only the single worst (largest) distance counts, because that worst point is what forces the circle to be big.

Where it's used — facility location. If you must place one fire station, hospital, cell tower, or warehouse so that the worst-served customer is as close as possible, that is exactly the 1-center: nobody should be more than the radius away.

A tiny concrete instance. Three points: A=(0,0), B=(4,0), C=(2,1). Standing at A makes the farthest point B, distance 4. Standing at the midpoint (2,0) drops the farthest distance to 2 (both A and B are exactly 2 away, and C is only ≈1.4 away). No spot does better — the smallest enclosing circle is centred at (2,0) with radius 2. Notice the answer is pinned by the two extreme points, not the middle one.

Watch it solved

Eight points (the dark dots). Watch the enclosing circle shrink and re-centre toward the smallest one that still covers everything. The red dot is the current farthest point (it sets the radius). Green dots are points pruned because they sit safely deep inside — they can never be the farthest, so the algorithm drops them. Step through.

How the solver works (prune-and-search). The slides solve 1-center in two stages. First the constrained version (§7.1): force the centre onto a given line, which is solvable in O(n) by pairing points, taking a median, and discarding the closer point of each pair. Then the general version (§7.2–7.3) uses that constrained solver plus a sign-determination test — "should the centre move up or down? left or right?" — to decide which side the true optimum lies on, and prunes ~n/16 of the points each round. Because a constant fraction vanishes every round, the work shrinks geometrically: T(n) = T(15n/16) + O(n) = O(n) — linear time.

Time: O(n) Prunes: ~n/16 each round Goal: min-max distance Brute base: ≤ 16 points
