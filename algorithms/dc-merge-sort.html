Merge Sort

Sort a list by splitting it in half, sorting each half, then merging the two sorted halves back together — the textbook divide-and-conquer sort.

What is it?

Divide and conquer is a strategy: take a big problem, break it into smaller copies of the same problem, solve those, and combine the answers. Merge sort applies this to sorting.

The idea: a list of one element is already sorted. So keep splitting the list in half until every piece has just one element. Then build back up — repeatedly merge pairs of sorted pieces into bigger sorted pieces, until the whole list is one sorted run.

To merge two already-sorted runs means: walk a finger along each run, and at each step take the smaller of the two front elements into the output. Because both inputs are sorted, this produces one correctly-sorted run in a single pass. Merging only works if both inputs are already sorted — that is the whole contract.

Tiny example: to merge [27, 38] and [10, 43] — compare 27 vs 10, take 10; compare 27 vs 43, take 27; compare 38 vs 43, take 38; then 43 is left, take it → [10, 27, 38, 43].
