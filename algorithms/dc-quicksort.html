Divide & Conquer

Quicksort

Sort by picking a pivot, shoving everything smaller to its left and everything larger to its right, then sorting each side the same way.

What is it?

You have an unsorted list of numbers and want them in increasing order. Quicksort does it with one repeated trick called partition. Pick one element as the pivot (here we always grab the last element of the range). Now walk through the rest and rearrange so that every value smaller than the pivot sits to its left, every value larger sits to its right, and finally drop the pivot into the gap between them.

After that one pass the pivot is in its final sorted position — it will never move again — even though neither side is sorted yet. That is the divide-and-conquer payoff: the problem splits into a left partition (all smaller) and a right partition (all larger), and you recurse — run the very same quicksort on each side. A range of size 0 or 1 is already sorted, so the recursion stops there.

Tiny example: partitioning [3, 1, 2] with pivot 2 gives [1, 2, 3] — the 1 moved left of the pivot, the 3 stayed right, and 2 landed in its final slot.

Avg time: O(n log n) Worst time: O(n²) Space: O(log n)
