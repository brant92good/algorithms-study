Hamiltonian Circuit

Given an undirected graph, does it contain a cycle that visits every vertex exactly once and returns to where it started? Search for one by growing a path and backtracking at dead ends.

📄 Source: Alg05 · Ch 6 Searching Strategies §2, Fig. 6-6 (the graph) & Fig. 6-8 (the search tree).

🧠 Solved by: Depth-First Search

The problem

You are given an undirected graph — a set of vertices (dots) joined by edges (lines). A Hamiltonian circuit is a closed tour that:

  • starts at some vertex,
  • walks along edges of the graph,
  • visits every vertex exactly once (no repeats, none skipped), and
  • returns to the starting vertex at the end.

The Hamiltonian circuit problem asks a yes/no question: does the given graph contain such a circuit at all? It is a feasibility problem (find one valid tour, or prove none exists) — not an optimization problem, so the edges carry no weights and there is no "cheapest" tour to compute.

Do not confuse it with an Euler circuit. An Euler circuit must use every edge exactly once (vertices may repeat); a Hamiltonian circuit must visit every vertex exactly once (edges may be left unused). They sound similar but are very different problems — Euler circuits have an easy rule (all vertices even degree), while deciding Hamiltonicity is NP-complete.

The concrete instance (Fig. 6-6). A graph on 7 vertices numbered 1–7, with these 9 edges:

1–2, 1–6, 1–3, 2–3, 6–5, 7–5, 7–4, 3–4, 4–5

Question: is there a circuit through all 7 vertices? We will search for one below — and find that 1 → 6 → 5 → 7 → 4 → 3 → 2 → 1 works (check: every neighbour pair is a real edge, all 7 vertices appear once, and it closes back to 1).
