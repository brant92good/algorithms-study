Dynamic Programming

0/1 Knapsack

Pick a subset of items to maximize value without overflowing a weight budget — solved by filling a table, one decision at a time.

What is it?

You have a knapsack (a bag) that can hold at most W units of weight. There are n items, each with a weight and a value. You want to choose a subset of items whose total weight fits in the bag and whose total value is as large as possible.

The "0/1" means each item is all-or-nothing: you either take it once (1) or leave it (0). You cannot take half an item, and you cannot take two copies. (That distinguishes it from the "fractional" or "unbounded" knapsack variants.)

The trick is dynamic programming: instead of trying all 2n subsets, we solve small subproblems and reuse the answers. Define

dp[i][w] = best total value using only the first i items, with a weight budget of w

For each item i (with weight wt and value val) and each budget w, we face one decision and take the better of two options:

  • Skip item i: the value is whatever we already had with the first i−1 items in the same budget — that's the cell directly above, dp[i-1][w].
  • Take item i (only if it fits, wt ≤ w): collect val, then spend the leftover budget on earlier items — dp[i-1][w-wt] + val. That source cell is up and to the left.

dp[i][w] = max( dp[i-1][w],   dp[i-1][w-wt] + val )

Row 0 (no items) is all zeros. We fill row by row; the final cell dp[n][W] in the bottom-right is the answer.

Tiny example. One item of weight 2, value 3, and a budget of 5: it fits, so dp[1][5] = max(0, 0+3) = 3. With a budget of 1 it doesn't fit, so dp[1][1] = 0.

Watch it run

Four items — item1(w 1, v 1), item2(w 3, v 4), item3(w 4, v 5), item4(w 5, v 7) — and capacity W = 7. Rows are "first i items"; columns are the budget 0…7. The gold cell is the one being computed; the two amber cells are the "skip" (above) and "take" (above-left) sources it chooses between.

Time: O(nW) Space: O(nW) Class: pseudo-polynomial
