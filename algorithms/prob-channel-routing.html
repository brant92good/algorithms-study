Channel Routing

A chip has pins lined up on the top and bottom of a corridor; wires connecting same-labelled pins must run along horizontal rows. Use as few rows as possible — and A* finds the minimum.

📄 Source: Alg05 · The Searching Strategies §12 (Channel Routing via A*; Fig. 6-40 / 6-46 / 6-48).

🧠 Solved by: A* Algorithm

The problem

In VLSI chip layout, a channel is a rectangular corridor between two rows of components. Along its top edge and its bottom edge sit terminals (pins). Each terminal carries a label, a small integer — pins that share a label belong to the same net and must all be electrically connected by wire.

  • A net = the group of all terminals (top and bottom) bearing the same label. Every net must be wired up.
  • A track = one horizontal row inside the channel along which a wire can run. A net's wire runs mostly along a single track, with short vertical "drops" up to its top/bottom pins.
  • Two nets may share a track only if their horizontal spans don't overlap — otherwise their wires would short together. That is an illegal wiring.

Goal: assign every net to a track so that no two overlapping nets land on the same track, and minimize the total number of tracks. Fewer tracks means a shorter channel and a smaller chip. This problem is NP-complete.

The concrete instance (Fig. 6-40). A channel with 13 columns. Each column lists its top pin and bottom pin (0 = no pin):

So net 1 spans from column 11 (top) to column 13 (bottom); net 7 from column 4 (top) to column 3 (bottom); net 8 from column 2 (top) to column 8 (bottom); and so on. A naïve feasible routing needs 7 tracks (one net each); the optimal routing fits everything into just 4 tracks.

Two helper graphs capture the constraints.

  • The Horizontal Constraint Graph (HCG): a node per net; a directed edge u → v means "if nets u and v ever share a track, u must sit to the left of v" — i.e. their spans conflict. Two nets joined in the HCG cannot freely overlap. A set of nets that mutually conflict is a clique; the maximal cliques here are {1,8}, {1,3,7}, and {5,7}. Each maximal clique is a bundle of nets that can be packed onto one track.
  • The Vertical Constraint Graph (VCG): an edge top-net → bottom-net wherever a column has one net on top and another on the bottom, forcing the top net into a track above the bottom net. Here the VCG is just 1→2, 3→4, 5→6 (e.g. "net 3 must be wired before net 4"); nets 7 and 8 are unconstrained vertically.

Watch it solved

First the HCG of this channel (nodes = nets, arrows = "must be left of"); the three maximal cliques are the candidate first tracks. Then A* searches the tree of track assignments with f = g + h, where g = the tree level (tracks committed so far) and h = the maximal local density (a lower bound on tracks still needed, so it never overestimates → A* is optimal).
