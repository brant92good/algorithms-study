Prune-and-Search

Two-Variable Linear Programming

Find the lowest point of a "valley" formed by a stack of straight lines — and throw away a quarter of the lines every round to reach the answer in linear time.

What is it?

Linear programming means: find the best (smallest or largest) value of some quantity, when your choices are limited by a list of straight-line rules. "Two-variable" means the choice is a point (x, y) in the plane. Here the goal is simple — make y as small as possible.

Each rule has the form y ≥ aᵢx + bᵢ. A single rule like that says: the point must sit on or above the line aᵢx + bᵢ — it carves the plane into a half-plane (everything above the line is allowed). With n rules, the allowed (feasible) region is everything above all the lines at once.

The floor of that region — the lowest y you're allowed to have at each x — is the pointwise maximum of the lines:

F(x) = max { a₁x+b₁, a₂x+b₂, …, aₙx+bₙ }

This F(x) is the upper envelope of the lines (you always take whichever line is highest). Because it is the max of straight lines, it is a convex "valley" shape: it comes down, hits a single bottom, and goes back up — it can never dip twice. So the answer x₀ is just the one lowest point of F, and y₀ = F(x₀).

The prune-and-search trick. Don't build all of F — that is wasteful. Instead pair the lines up, intersect each pair, and pick the median intersection xₘ as a test point. Look at the slope of F right at xₘ: if it slopes up, the bottom is to the left (x₀ < xₘ); if it slopes down, the bottom is to the right; if it is flat there, you're standing on the answer. Knowing the side lets you delete the loser of each pair on the wrong side — about n/4 lines vanish every round. Repeat on what's left. Since each round does O(n) work and shrinks the input by a constant fraction, the total is O(n).
