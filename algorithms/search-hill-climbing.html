Hill Climbing

A greedy depth-first search that always walks toward the best-looking neighbour — fast, but it can march straight into a dead end and freeze.

What is it?

First, the puzzle. The 8-puzzle is a 3×3 board holding eight numbered tiles (1–8) and one empty square (the blank). You may slide any tile that sits next to the blank into it — which is the same as moving the blank up, down, left, or right. The aim is to reach a fixed goal arrangement:

goal: 1 2 3 8 _ 4 7 6 5

Searching for the right sequence of slides is a tree problem: each board is a node, and its children are the boards you can reach in one slide. To decide which child to try, hill climbing scores every node with an evaluation function:

f(n) = d(n) + w(n) d(n) = depth — how many moves you have already made to reach n w(n) = number of misplaced tiles — tiles not yet in their goal spot

Lower f is better. Hill climbing is a greedy variant of depth-first search: from the current board it generates all neighbours, then commits to the single neighbour with the smallest f and dives deeper from there. It never keeps a list of the other open nodes — it has only a local view of the current board's own children.

That greed is the catch. If every neighbour scores worse than (or no better than) where you are — a local optimum — hill climbing has nowhere good to step and no saved alternatives to back up to. It gets stuck, even when a better path existed earlier in the tree.
