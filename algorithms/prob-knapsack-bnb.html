0/1 Knapsack — Branch & Bound

Pack a knapsack of capacity M with whole items (take it or leave it) to maximize profit — searching a tree of "in / out" decisions, but using bounds to prune most of it away.

📄 Source: Alg05 · The Searching Strategies §10 (0/1 Knapsack via Branch-and-Bound), Fig. 6-27 (branching) & Fig. 6-28 (the solved tree); example n = 6, M = 34.

🧠 Solved by: Branch-and-Bound (also by Dynamic Programming)

The problem

You have n items. Each item i has a weight Wᵢ and a profit Pᵢ (both positive integers). You have a knapsack that can carry a total weight of at most M (the capacity). For every item you make one yes/no choice — a decision variable Xᵢ ∈ {0, 1}: Xᵢ = 1 means "put item i in the bag," Xᵢ = 0 means "leave it out." You cannot take a fraction of an item — it is whole-or-nothing (that is the "0/1" in the name).

The goal is to pick the subset of items that maximizes total profit without overloading the bag:

maximize   P₁X₁ + P₂X₂ + … + PₙXₙ   (total profit)
subject to  W₁X₁ + W₂X₂ + … + WₙXₙ ≤ M   (fits in the bag)
with each   Xᵢ = 0 or 1

The concrete instance (Fig. 6-28). n = 6 items, capacity M = 34. The items are listed in non-increasing profit density (Pᵢ / Wᵢ), which the bounding step relies on:

item i │ 1 2 3 4 5 6 ────────┼────────────────────────────────── profit P│ 6 10 4 5 6 4 weight W│ 10 19 8 10 12 8 P / W │ 0.60 0.53 0.50 0.50 0.50 0.50

One quirk of these slides: branch-and-bound is naturally a minimization machine (it prunes nodes whose bound is too high). So the deck negates the profit and minimizes −Σ PᵢXᵢ. A profit of 17 becomes the objective value −17; "more profit" means "more negative," and the best (optimal) value is the smallest (most negative) one. Keep that sign flip in mind — every bound below is a negative number.

Watch it solved

Branch-and-bound builds a binary tree (Fig. 6-27): at level i the left edge is Xᵢ = 1 (take item i), the right edge is Xᵢ = 0 (skip it). At each node it computes two numbers, then prunes:

  • U.B. (upper bound) = the value of some feasible whole-item packing — a solution we could actually accept. The best U.B. seen so far is our "best yet."
  • L.B. (lower bound) = the value of the fractional relaxation (allow 0 ≤ Xᵢ ≤ 1, fill greedily by density, take a fraction of the last item). Because fractions can only help, this L.B. is the best the subtree could ever reach — no integer packing under this node can beat it.
