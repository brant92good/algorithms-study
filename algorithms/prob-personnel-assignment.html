Personnel Assignment

Hand n jobs to n people at minimum total cost — but the jobs come with a precedence order, so not every assignment is legal. Search the legal ones cleverly with a cost lower bound that prunes whole subtrees.

📄 Source: Alg05 · The Searching Strategies §8 (Personnel Assignment by Branch-and-Bound; Tables 6-1 / 6-2, Fig. 6-21).

🧠 Solved by: Branch-and-Bound

The problem

You have n people and n jobs, and you must give each person exactly one job and each job exactly one person (a one-to-one assignment). Assigning person Pᵢ to job Jⱼ costs Cᵢⱼ — these costs are given in a table. The naive goal would be "pick the cheapest one-to-one assignment," but here there is a twist.

The jobs carry a partial order — a precedence relation. Some jobs must come before others; some pairs of jobs are unrelated (incomparable). The people, by contrast, are linearly ordered: P₁ < P₂ < … < Pₙ. The rule that makes an assignment feasible is that it must respect the job order:

If person Pᵢ comes before person Pⱼ (i.e. Pᵢ < Pⱼ), then the job given to Pᵢ must come before-or-equal to the job given to Pⱼ in the job order. In short: a feasible assignment lists the jobs in a topological order of the precedence relation as you read down the person list P₁, P₂, …, Pₙ.

Objective: minimize the total cost Σᵢ Σⱼ Cᵢⱼ·Xᵢⱼ, where Xᵢⱼ = 1 if Pᵢ is assigned to Jⱼ and 0 otherwise, subject to each person/job used once and the feasibility (precedence) rule.

The concrete instance (n = 4)

Four people P₁…P₄, four jobs J₁…J₄. The cost matrix (Table 6-1) — rows are people, columns are jobs:

Person \ Job J1 J2 J3 J4 P1 29 19 17 12 P2 32 30 26 28 P3 3 21 7 9 P4 18 13 10 15

The job precedence (Fig. 6-21) is two simple chains — J1 must precede J3, and J2 must precede J4:

J1 J2 | | v v J3 J4

J1 and J2 are incomparable; so are J3 and J4. The legal (topologically sorted) job sequences are therefore:

J1, J2, J3, J4 J1, J2, J4, J3 J1, J3, J2, J4 J2, J1, J3, J4 J2, J1, J4, J3

An assignment is feasible iff the jobs handed to P₁, P₂, P₃, P₄ (in that order) form one of these sequences. For example P1→J1, P2→J2, P3→J3, P4→J4 is feasible (sequence J1,J2,J3,J4). The question: among all feasible assignments, which is cheapest?

Watch it solved

Two stages. First we reduce the cost matrix (subtract a constant from every row and every column so each has a zero) — the total subtracted is a guaranteed lower bound on any assignment. Then we branch: a decision tree picks P₁'s job, then P₂'s, and so on, carrying a running cost and using the bound to prune. Step through.
